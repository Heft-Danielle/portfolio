STEAM Challenge

Northview Public Schools & Shelby Public Schools

The STEAM Challenge curriculum is a hands-on, project-based engineering and design course that I developed and scaled across multiple districts. The course emphasizes student-centered learning through real-world builds—such as cars, catapults, and boats—while integrating principles of science, technology, engineering, arts, and mathematics.

Key Features:

  • Project-Based Learning: Students engage in iterative design challenges that promote critical thinking, creativity, and collaboration.
  • Scalable Design: Expanded from a single-section pilot to a two-part course sequence (Challenge A/B), consistently running five sections per term with a waitlist.
  • Standards-Aligned: Fully aligned to Common Core State Standards in Mathematics, incorporating engineering design practices and cross-disciplinary rigor.
  • Student Feedback Loop: Annually updated based on student reflections and performance data to ensure engagement, relevance, and continuous improvement.
  • Equity & Engagement: Designed to be inclusive and adaptable, supporting a wide range of learners through differentiated instruction and authentic problem-solving.

This curriculum exemplifies my approach to experiential learning, curriculum innovation, and student empowerment through real-world application.

Projects

Catapult

Students will design and build a catapult to compete in both distance and accuracy challenges. It must launch at least 10 feet for one event and land in a hula-hoop at 5 feet for the other, with competitions for the farthest and most accurate launches.

Rubber Band Car

Students will design and build a car powered entirely by rubber bands or balloons, demonstrating motion without any human assistance. To meet the challenge, the car must travel at least 10 feet, with a competition to test the design that travels the farthest distance.

Carnival Games

Students will design a new carnival game by combining elements of existing games or inventing an original concept. The game must be appropriately challenging for high school students and adults, include clear win/lose conditions, and feature themed decorations.

Board Games

Students will design a themed, interactive board game for 2–6 players that includes a physical build component and can be learned and played within 30 minutes. The game must be neat, colorful, and include a complete typed rule set, with peer ratings based on fun and playability.

Boats

Students will build boats using only cardboard and masking tape, designed to hold two students and float in a lake. Each team must also create two oars, with the goal of staying afloat the longest to win the challenge. This project was featured on the School News Network

Common Core Math Practices

  • Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them. Understand the problem, plan a solution pathway, and keep working until the problem is solved.
  • Reason abstractly and quantitatively. Translate between real-world contexts and mathematical symbols; make sense of quantities and their relationships.
  • Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others. Use logic and evidence to justify solutions and analyze others’ arguments.
  • Model with mathematics. Use mathematics to represent and analyze real-world situations.
  • Use appropriate tools strategically. Select and use tools like graphing calculators, software, or rulers effectively.
  • Attend to precision. Communicate clearly, use accurate calculations, and specify units and labels correctly.
  • Look for and make use of structure. Identify patterns or structures to simplify complex problems.
  • Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning. Notice repeated calculations or processes and use this to solve problems more efficiently.
